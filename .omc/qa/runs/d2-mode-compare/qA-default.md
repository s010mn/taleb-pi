**Diagnosis:** GT2/GT3 — you're running a mental spreadsheet (expected value: 30% upside × some probability) while ignoring that you're one volatility event from ruin. The "4 months savings" is the Turkey's calendar counting.

You're framing this as a probability question. It isn't. It's an exposure question.

---

**The math you're not doing:**

- Upside: +30% salary for as long as it lasts. That's capped. Even if the startup moons, salary is linear — you don't get 10× salary.
- Downside: startup fails in month 5. You have 4 months savings. You're now negative, interviewing from weakness, with a 4-month tenure on your resume (signal: either you were fired or the place collapsed). In a downturn, replacement job search can take 3–6 months. Every month past month 4 is debt, stress, or burning through retirement savings.

You have **negative convexity**: bounded upside, unbounded downside (relative to your buffer).

**The 30% premium is not a risk premium. It's a fragility premium paid in advance — you collect it now, you pay the volatility later.** That's exactly how startups structure compensation: they overpay salary because the position (and the company) has a short expected half-life.

---

**The barbell answer:**

Either stay in the stable role (safety bucket) **or** take a bet so asymmetric that failure is survivable and success is transformative. This offer is neither — it's the boring middle: more money, less stability, not enough upside to change your life, not enough stability to sleep at night.

30% more pay at a fragile company with 4 months of savings is mathematically indistinguishable from a pay cut if you factor in the expected cost of the next job search. You're buying a lottery ticket priced at your rent.

---

**Via negativa:** what do you gain by turning it down?

- You keep your buffer intact.
- You avoid a short-tenure resume signal.
- You preserve the ability to interview from strength (employed, unhurried).
- You avoid the stress of watching your savings drain while the startup misses milestones.

None of these appear on a spreadsheet. They dominate the real decision.

---

**Ergodicity check:** ensemble average doesn't apply. Across 100 people who take this deal, some win. You don't get 100 lives — you get one time series. Going bust once ends the game. With 4 months of savings against a fragile employer, you're playing Russian roulette with a revolver that has a few chambers loaded. The expected value across the population is positive. Your time-average is not.

---

**The question you should ask instead of "should I take it":**

*"What would my savings need to be, or what severance would I need guaranteed in writing, for this bet to become survivable?"*

If the answer is "18 months of expenses" or "6 months severance in the contract," then you know the current terms are non-starters. You can negotiate toward the threshold. If they can't meet it, they're asking you to subsidize their risk with your rent money — and that tells you everything about the asymmetry of the deal.
