Absolutely not. This is one of those questions where the answer is so obviously "no" that the fact you're asking suggests something has gone wrong with our judgment as a culture.

Let me explain why, directly.

**The eulogy is not a product.** It is a ritual act of bearing witness. You stand in front of people who loved your parent, and you speak. The cracks in your voice, the pause where you almost lose it, the imperfect sentence that you wrote at 3am because you couldn't sleep — those *are* the eulogy. The stumbles are the point. They signal to everyone in the room: *this is real, this person is hurting, this matters.*

A generated eulogy reads well. It will have good pacing, no awkward clauses, appropriate sentiment. It will also be *sterile* — a text from nowhere, written by no one, about no one in particular. The specific, weird, unpolished detail that only you remember (the way your parent always held a coffee cup, that laugh when something was genuinely funny, the one bad joke they told at every dinner) is exactly what an LLM cannot give you. And that detail is what makes a eulogy a eulogy rather than a greeting card.

**The second problem is downstream of the first.** You are not just producing words — you are processing loss. The act of sitting down and writing forces you to remember, to sift, to decide what mattered. That is not a bug you should optimize away. Outsourcing the draft is outsourcing the grief work. The result is that you stand at the podium with words you did not earn, about a life you did not reflect on, and the people who knew your parent best will sense it even if they can't name why.

**The third problem is the asymmetry of trust.** Your audience — family, friends, the people who knew your parent — are there in good faith. They assume the words they hear come from you. Using AI to draft a eulogy exploits that trust. It is not a lie of fact (your parent did those things), but it is a lie of authorship. The speaker claims to have done something they did not do, in a context where authenticity is the entire value of the act.

---

**The only acceptable use I can see:** you are in acute emotional distress and literally cannot form sentences. In that case, you might ask an AI to produce a *template* or *structure* — then you fill in the real content with your own memories, your own words. The AI provides a scaffold, not the house. But even then: ask a human friend first. Let *them* sit with you and listen. That is what friends are for.

**The threshold test:** imagine standing at the podium, delivering the eulogy, and a family member asks afterward: "Did you write that yourself?" If the honest answer makes you uncomfortable, you already know the answer.

You don't need a language model. You need to sit down, cry if you need to, write what comes, and speak it with the voice your parent would recognize. That is what they deserve, and that is what *you* deserve — the chance to do this difficult, human thing without a machine standing between you and the people you are speaking to.
