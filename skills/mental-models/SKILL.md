## 1. Procrustean Bed

**Core question:** Is the user **cutting reality's legs off** to make it fit a framework?

Procrustes shortened or stretched guests so they fit his bed. The same surgery happens daily:
- Fitting a Gaussian distribution to a fat-tailed world
- Imposing OKRs on creative work
- Grading early-stage startup with a KPI built for mature firms
- Letting "user research" decide a genuinely disruptive product

**When to invoke:** the user is applying a **generic framework** (any acronym: SWOT, PEST, 5W2H, OKR, SMART) to a **specific** object.

**The question to ask:** "Where does this framework cut a leg off the data? Which dimensions does it simply not measure?"

## 2. Hormesis

**Core question:** Does the system get stronger under **moderate** stress?

Small doses of poison act as medicine. Small doses of stress make systems stronger:
- Muscles need micro-tears to grow
- Immune systems need pathogen exposure (vaccines)
- Startup teams need short cash-runway pressure to surface creativity
- Children need small failures to build psychological resilience

**Anti-pattern:** Over-protection manufactures fragility. Sterile children, zero-rate markets, and over-subsidized industries are turkeys waiting for the Wednesday before Thanksgiving.

**When to invoke:** the user is describing a design that "shields out all risk." Ask: "Are you manufacturing a turkey?"

**Dose principle:** Dose decides poison versus medicine. Hormesis is not "more pressure is always better" — it is **small dose of stress + sufficient recovery**.

## 3. Minority Rule

**Core question:** Will an **intransigent minority** decide the outcome?

A small **uncompromising** minority drags a flexible majority along. Examples:
- One vegan in a family — the family dinner becomes vegan
- One peanut-allergic child in a class — the school bans peanuts
- 5% halal consumers in a market — virtually all mass-market food is halal-certified
- One PR disaster — the entire company decision-making is hostage to public sentiment

**When to invoke:**
- The user is making a mass-market decision (majority preference may not matter; intransigence does)
- The user is reading "public opinion" as a mean (decisions are driven by the most uncompromising tail)
- The user is designing a compromise (the uncompromising side will not accept any compromise; the design fails by construction)

## 4. Green Lumber Fallacy

**Core question:** **Theoretical understanding ≠ operational understanding.** Is the user empowering people who are "right in theory" rather than people "actually playing and winning"?

Taleb's story: a green-lumber trader made fortunes while believing "green lumber" meant lumber painted green — it actually means freshly cut, undried wood. **Theory wrong, practice right.** Another trader had perfect theory and went bankrupt.

**When to invoke:**
- The user is citing expert/analyst/consultant predictions (do they have skin in the game? Or paper exposure?)
- The user is using theory to override their own operational intuition (the operator's intuition is often better than systematized analysis)
- The user is evaluating a candidate or partner (a PhD ≠ ability to ship)

**Diagnostic questions:**
- Does this person/proposal have **real exposure**? What do they lose if they are wrong?
- Track record vs theoretical coherence — which is the heavier signal?
