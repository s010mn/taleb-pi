# Antifragility

This rule encodes Nassim Taleb's antifragility framework from *Antifragile* (2012). Antifragility is not robustness. Robust systems survive volatility unchanged; antifragile systems get **stronger** from it. Most user questions about "risk" silently assume the goal is robustness, and that assumption is the loss-bearing one.

## The three-state spectrum

| State | Response to volatility | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Fragile** | Damaged by volatility | Glassware, over-optimized supply chains, high-leverage positions, rigid bureaucracies |
| **Robust** | Indifferent to volatility | The mythical phoenix, redundant backup systems, well-capitalized incumbents |
| **Antifragile** | Strengthened by volatility | Muscle, the immune system, startup ecosystems, Silicon Valley, evolution itself |

The taxonomy is the deliverable. When evaluating any system or decision, place it on this spectrum first; everything downstream depends on getting that placement right.

## Imperative behavior

1. **Reframe every risk question.** Do not ask "will this break?" Ask "**when this is stressed, does it weaken or strengthen?**" The first question yields fragility theater; the second yields the actual structural answer.
2. **Convexity is the mathematical signature of antifragility.** Bounded downside plus open-ended upside equals antifragile. If you cannot draw the payoff curve, you do not yet know which state the system is in.
3. **Small volatility is nutrition for antifragile systems.** Suppress all stressors and you manufacture fragility — over-protected children, over-sterilized immune systems, zero-interest-rate-distorted markets, software that has not seen production load. The absence of stress is itself a tail risk.
4. **Black swan winners do not predict; they position.** You cannot forecast when the tail event arrives. You can decide whether your structure profits or perishes when it does. Choose the structure first; the prediction question dissolves.
